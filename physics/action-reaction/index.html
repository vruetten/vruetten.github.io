Law of action and reaction





We call the phenomenon of "exchanging of momentum”: “force”.

I just finished talking about the definition of “force”, but “force” doesn't actually exist. It is just a concept that we use for the phenomenon of a change in momentum - we then say that a “force is acting upon X”. Do you feel you understand that?

For example, when two moving objects collide with each other, these objects must change their motion, but what we observe is just the fact that motion has changed. We cannot see the force, nor does force "pop out" from anywhere. When observing the change of motion, we just indirectly assume that “a force acted at the interface of these two objects”.

This simply means that we decided to assume that “force which makes momentum change” acts between objects. We just take the idea for granted, as a fact.

We then call this phenomenon of "exchanging of momentum” as “force”.

So why is it necessary to introduce this concept? The answer is that it is convenient and intuitive for human beings. "She is forcing the pen to move.” “May the force with you”. Those are common phrases we can hear in our daily lives. These common phrases and the physical concept of “force” have some similarity.

If we use this concept, “force”, we can easily summarize the effects between objects.

“An object, which exerts force, receives the same amplitude of force with opposite direction.”

This is “law of action and reaction”. It is little bit hard to explain it without using the idea of “force". In fact, conservation of momentum, which I will explain in next article, is the same concept as this.

Also, if “force” is defined, it is useful for the field of “statics”, which concerns itself with centers of mass center and objects in equilibria, and the idea is common in “electromagnetism”, too. This concept, “force”, is applicable in many broad areas, and it is meaningful that dynamics is translated as the “learning of forces” in Japanese. It is definitely true that “force” is the main theme of dynamics.

Dynamics, static mechanics and kinematics are all translated to Japanese in same words. “Learning of force”. I don’t know how to use them properly, but it is better to follow the traditional way of defining them in each field.
